How to Calculate Aquarium Volume and Salt Mix

Knowing the exact volume of your aquarium is critical for everything from dosing medications to mixing saltwater. Guess wrong, and you risk overdosing fish treatments, creating toxic salinity swings, or wasting expensive salt mix. This guide walks through the math for every common tank shape and explains how to get your salt mixing right.

Volume Formulas by Tank Shape

Rectangular Tanks

The most common aquarium shape. The formula is straightforward:

Volume (in³) = Length × Width × Height
US Gallons = Volume (in³) ÷ 231

A standard 55-gallon tank measures 48" × 12" × 20", which gives 11,520 in³ ÷ 231 = 49.9 gallons. Note that manufacturer-listed sizes are approximate — always calculate from actual dimensions.

Cylindrical Tanks

Column tanks and drum-style tanks use the cylinder formula:

Volume (in³) = π × (Diameter / 2)² × Height

A 18" diameter × 24" tall cylinder holds about 6,107 in³ = 26.4 gallons.

Bow-Front Tanks

Bow-front aquariums have a curved front panel that adds extra volume. The exact curve varies by manufacturer, but a reasonable approximation adds about 10-15% over a standard rectangle of the same dimensions:

Volume ≈ Length × Center Width × Height × 0.85

The 0.85 factor accounts for the bow shape averaging between the maximum center width and the narrower edges. This gives a result within 5% of actual volume for most bow-front designs.

Pro Tip: The calculated volume is the tank's total capacity, but your actual water volume will be 10-15% less due to substrate, rocks, and equipment taking up space. For medication dosing, use 85% of calculated volume to be safe.

Saltwater Mixing: Getting It Right

For saltwater and reef aquariums, precise salinity is essential. Most marine fish thrive at 35 parts per thousand (ppt), which corresponds to a specific gravity of about 1.026 on a hydrometer.

How Much Salt Per Gallon?

The amount varies by brand, but a good rule of thumb:

  • ½ cup of salt mix per gallon gets you to approximately 35 ppt
  • That's about 135 grams (4.75 oz) per gallon
  • For a 50-gallon tank: roughly 25 cups or 15 lbs of salt mix

Always mix salt in a separate container with a powerhead running for at least 24 hours before adding it to your tank. Never add dry salt directly to a tank with livestock.

Common Mistakes

1. Measuring the Outside of the Tank

Glass thickness matters. A tank with 3/8" glass on each side loses almost an inch of interior width. For large tanks, measure the inside dimensions for accurate volume calculations.

2. Forgetting Displacement

A reef tank with 50 lbs of live rock displaces roughly 2-3 gallons of water. A 3-inch sand bed in a 48" × 12" tank displaces about 6 gallons. Your actual water volume is always less than the empty tank calculation.

3. Mixing Salt to the Wrong Temperature

Salt dissolves and reads differently at different temperatures. Always mix and measure salinity at the same temperature your tank runs (typically 76-80°F / 24-27°C). Cold freshwater with salt added will read differently once it warms up.

Watch Out: Never trust a hydrometer alone for salinity readings. Swing-arm hydrometers are notoriously inaccurate. Invest in a digital refractometer ($30-50) for reliable readings. Calibrate it with 35 ppt reference solution monthly.

Water Change Calculations

Regular water changes are the foundation of aquarium health. When doing a 20% water change on a 50-gallon tank, you're replacing 10 gallons — so mix 10 gallons of new saltwater, not 50. Our calculator gives you the total volume, and you can easily calculate any percentage change from there.

Quick Reference: Standard Tank Sizes

  • 10 gallon: 20" × 10" × 12" (great starter freshwater)
  • 20 gallon long: 30" × 12" × 12" (nano reef capable)
  • 40 gallon breeder: 36" × 18" × 16" (popular reef size)
  • 55 gallon: 48" × 12" × 20" (classic community tank)
  • 75 gallon: 48" × 18" × 20" (serious reef territory)
  • 125 gallon: 72" × 18" × 22" (dream tank)
